2-game vs best-of-1: who makes top 64?

A tournament simulation · 2040 players · 9 Swiss rounds · cut to top 64

The setup

Imagine one big tournament where half the field (1020 players) plays a 2-game format worth 3 points per game won, and the other half plays best-of-1 worth 6 points per game. Everyone plays 9 rounds, then the top 64 on points make the cut.

To strip out every confound, this model assumes no skill differences at all — every game is a coin flip. The only thing that ever changes is a going-first advantage in the 2-game format, which you control below. That isolates exactly what the format structure does on its own.

Why this happens

Best-of-1 is decisive. Every match ends 6–0. To go undefeated you win 9 coin flips — rare, but it happens to about 2 players per 1020 every time. The records spread out cleanly: some players run hot, some run cold, and the hot ones fill the top of the standings.

The 2-game format manufactures ties. When you go first in game 1 and your opponent goes first in game 2, going-first advantage pulls both games toward the player who started them — which means more and more matches end 1-1, worth just 3 points each. A split isn't a win and isn't a loss; it's a wash that drags both players toward the middle of the pack.

The takeaway

Model: 2040 players, two independent 9-round Swiss pools, every game a coin flip modified only by the going-first advantage shown. No skill variable, no game-win tiebreaker — players are ranked on match points alone, with ties at the cut line broken at random. Each data point is averaged over 4,000 simulated tournaments. Going first in best-of-1 has no effect here because, with no skill, a coin flip stays a coin flip regardless of who starts.
